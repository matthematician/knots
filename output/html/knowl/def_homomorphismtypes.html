Definition 2.1.12.

Let \(G,H\) be two groups and \(f \colon G \to H\) be a homomorphism.

We say that \(f\) is a trivial homomorphism if, for all \(x\in G\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} f(x) = e, \text{ where }e\text{ is the identity of }H. \end{equation*}

We say that \(f\) is one-to-one if, for all \(x,y\in G\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} \text{If }x\neq y\text{ then } f(x)\neq f(y). \end{equation*}

We say that \(f\) is onto if, for all \(h \in H\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \text{there exists }g \in G\text{ such that }h = f(g). \end{equation*}

If \(f \colon G \to H\) is both one-to-one and onto, we say \(f\) is an isomorphism.

For the (abelian) group of integers under addition, \((\mathbb{Z},+)\) the function \(f\colon \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}\) defined by \(f(x) = 5x\) is a homomorphism. This is because, for all integers \(x,y\text{,}\) we have

\begin{align*} f(x+y) \amp= 5(x+y)\\ \amp=5x + 5y\\ \amp=f(x) + f(y). \end{align*}

If \(G = (\mathbb{Z},+)\) is the group of integers with the operation of addition and \(H = (\mathbb{Q}^+,\cdot)\) is the group of positive rational numbers with the operation of multiplication, then

\begin{equation*} f\colon \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Q}^+, \text{ defined } f(n) = 2^n, \end{equation*}

is a group homomorphism. To see why, observe where the operations in each group show up in the following verification:

\begin{align*} f\bigl( x + y \bigr) \amp= 2^{x+y} \amp\amp \text{definition of }f\\ \amp= 2^x2^y \amp\amp \text{properties of exponents}\\ \amp= 2^x\cdot 2^y \amp\amp\text{multiplication is our operation in }\mathbb{Q}^+\\ \amp= f(x) \cdot f(y). \end{align*}

For the permutation group on \(n\) symbols \(G = (S_n,\circ)\) we can define a homomorphism \(f \colon G \to G\) by \(f(\sigma) = (12)\circ \sigma \circ (12)\text{.}\) For example, we can check -- using braid-a-grams, for example -- that \(f\bigl( (235) \bigr) = (12)\circ (235) \circ (12) = (135)\text{.}\) This is a homomorphism since, for any two permutations \(\sigma,\tau\in S_n\text{,}\) we have

\begin{align*} f(\sigma) \circ f(\tau) \amp= (12)\circ \sigma\circ \overbrace{(12)\, \circ \, (12)}^\epsilon \circ \tau \circ (12) \\ \amp= (12)\circ \sigma \circ \epsilon \circ \tau \circ (12)\\ \amp= (12) \circ \sigma\circ\tau \circ (12)\\ \amp= f\bigl( \sigma\circ \tau \bigr). \end{align*}

Remark 4.1.16.

The arcs of a knot diagram are generators for its fundamental kei, but in general they are not its only elements. There may be words we can write using these generators and the kei operation \(\triangleright\) which do not correspond to arcs in the knot diagram at all.

For example, in the video below we begin the construction of the fundamental kei of the figure-eight knot \(4_1\text{.}\) With the idempotent property we can fill in four entries in the operation table for this kei; the four crossings in the diagram give us eight further entries. However, this leaves four entries in the table undetermined. For example, \(w\triangleright x\) does not evaluate to any of the four arcs \(w,x,y,z\) of this diagram because there is no crossing at which \(x\) is the overcrossing arc and \(w\) one of its undercrossing arcs. So \(w\triangleright x\text{,}\) while it is an element of the fundamental kei, is not one of the diagram's arcs.

Indeed, the fundamental kei of the figure-eight knot can be shown to have eight elements,

\begin{equation*} \mathcal{K}(4_1) = \bigl\{ w, x, y, z, \; w\triangleright x, x\triangleright y, y\triangleright z, z\triangleright w \; \bigr\} . \end{equation*}

The three kei properties can then be used to fill in all 64 entries in its operation table. For example, we can compute

\begin{align*} (z\triangleright w) \triangleright w \amp= z \amp\amp \text{(kei property 2)} \end{align*}

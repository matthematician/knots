Section 2.1 Braids

Objectives

  1. Compare and contrast the algebraic structure of permutation groups and braid groups.

The “braid-a-gram” diagrams you sketched in Section 1.2 were our first hint of a connection between algebra (permutations) and knot-like diagrams. Trouble is, braid-a-grams aren't really knot-like, because their strands intersect one another at their crossings, something that physical strings can't do. In this section we'll fix that limitation, and then work out what happens to the algebra.

Definition 2.1.1.

A braid on \(n\) strands is a braid-a-gram, each of whose crossings is designated as either a positive crossing (\(\backslash\) over \(/\)) or a negative crossing (\(/\) over \(\backslash\)).

A braid whose crossings are all positive is called a positive braid.

We can use technology to both visualize and, later, perform algebra using braids. Execute the Sage cell below to visualize an example of a positive braid.

There is a one-to-one correspondence between positive braids and braid-a-grams. Given a positive braid, we can “flatten” all the crossings to make it a braid-a-gram; conversely, given a braid-a-gram, we can make each crossing positive to turn it into a positive braid.

Accordingly, we can borrow the notation we used for adjacent transpositions to develop notation for braids as follows.

Definition 2.1.4.

In a braid on \(n\) strands, for each \(1 \leq i \leq n-1\) we denote by \(s_i\) the positive crossing of the \(i\)-th strand over the \((i+1)\)-st strand.

Definition 2.1.5.

If \(b_1,b_2\) are two braids on \(n\) strands, we construct the composition \(b_1b_2\) by placing \(b_2\) directly beneath, and as a continuation of, \(b_1\) in the braid diagram.

The trivial braid \(e\) consists of a braid diagram with no crossings.

While Activity 2.1.2 highlights just how small a change is necessary to transform the algebraic structure of permutations (braid-a-grams) into positive braids, Activity 2.1.3 and Activity 2.1.4 point out that this small change has far-reaching consequences. Clearly braids are significantly different animals than permutations, so let's explore how to work in those differences.

First, to put braids and permutations on an even footing, algebraic-structure-wise, we'll need to remedy the deficiency you discovered in Activity 2.1.2 so we can promote braids to the structure of a group.

Definition 2.1.7.

For \(n\geq 2\text{,}\) the (Artin) braid group on \(n\) strands is the set of compositions of the generators

\begin{equation*} s_1,s_2,\ldots,s_{n-1} \end{equation*}

and their inverses

\begin{equation*} s_1^{-1}, s_2^{-1}, \ldots, s_{n-1}^{-1} \end{equation*}

which are simplified by the braid relations:

\begin{align} s_is_j \amp= s_js_i \amp\amp\text{whenever }|i-j|\geq 2\label{eq_braidcommute}\tag{2.1.1}\\ s_is_{i+1}s_i \amp= s_{i+1}s_is_{i+1} \amp\amp\text{for all }1 \leq i \leq n-2.\label{eq_skeinrelation}\tag{2.1.2} \end{align}

We denote the braid group on \(n\) strands by \(B_n\text{.}\)

As you might expect, the inverse generators represent negative crossings in a braid.

So now, we can simplify a braid using algebra (i.e., the simplification rules (2.1.1) and (2.1.2)). What's more, this simplification also has a meaning in geometric space that we'll make precise here.

Definition 2.1.8.

Let \(b_1,b_2\) be two braids on \(n\) strands. We say that \(b_1\) and \(b_2\) are isotopic, and write \(b_1 \simeq b_2\text{,}\) if one of them can be smoothly deformed into the other without detaching or breaking any strands.

Two braids are isotopic, in other words, if we could rearrange a physical model of one of them to produce the other, without cutting or detaching any strands in the process. This should feel “right” to our mathematical sensibilities, since if we are to study braids we should try to see past superficial differences (such as how we push the strands around in physical space) and see the intrinsic structure (i.e., how the strands are “really” tangled up with one another).

We conclude by making more precise the idea of comparing braids to permutations, as follows.

Definition 2.1.9.

Let \((G,\circ)\) and \((H,*)\) be two groups, and let \(f\colon G \to H\) be a function.

We call \(f\) a (group) homomorphism if, for all elements \(x,y\in G\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} f\bigl( x\circ y \bigr) = f(x) * f(y) . \end{equation*}

One way of thinking of a group homomorphism is that it gives us a choice: given two elements in \(G\text{,}\) I can either perform the operation of \(G\) on them first, before applying the function, i.e., calculate \(f(\, x\circ y \,)\text{,}\) or I can apply the function to each element first and then perform the operation of \(H\) on the result, i.e., \(f(x) * f(y)\text{.}\) The two results always agree.

Example 2.1.11. Some examples of group homomorphisms.

For the (abelian) group of integers under addition, \((\mathbb{Z},+)\) the function \(f\colon \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}\) defined by \(f(x) = 5x\) is a homomorphism. This is because, for all integers \(x,y\text{,}\) we have

\begin{align*} f(x+y) \amp= 5(x+y)\\ \amp=5x + 5y\\ \amp=f(x) + f(y). \end{align*}

If \(G = (\mathbb{Z},+)\) is the group of integers with the operation of addition and \(H = (\mathbb{Q}^+,\cdot)\) is the group of positive rational numbers with the operation of multiplication, then

\begin{equation*} f\colon \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Q}^+, \text{ defined } f(n) = 2^n, \end{equation*}

is a group homomorphism. To see why, observe where the operations in each group show up in the following verification:

\begin{align*} f\bigl( x + y \bigr) \amp= 2^{x+y} \amp\amp \text{definition of }f\\ \amp= 2^x2^y \amp\amp \text{properties of exponents}\\ \amp= 2^x\cdot 2^y \amp\amp\text{multiplication is our operation in }\mathbb{Q}^+\\ \amp= f(x) \cdot f(y). \end{align*}

For the permutation group on \(n\) symbols \(G = (S_n,\circ)\) we can define a homomorphism \(f \colon G \to G\) by \(f(\sigma) = (12)\circ \sigma \circ (12)\text{.}\) For example, we can check -- using braid-a-grams, for example -- that \(f\bigl( (235) \bigr) = (12)\circ (235) \circ (12) = (135)\text{.}\) This is a homomorphism since, for any two permutations \(\sigma,\tau\in S_n\text{,}\) we have

\begin{align*} f(\sigma) \circ f(\tau) \amp= (12)\circ \sigma\circ \overbrace{(12)\, \circ \, (12)}^\epsilon \circ \tau \circ (12) \\ \amp= (12)\circ \sigma \circ \epsilon \circ \tau \circ (12)\\ \amp= (12) \circ \sigma\circ\tau \circ (12)\\ \amp= f\bigl( \sigma\circ \tau \bigr). \end{align*}

Morally speaking, a homomorphism is a way of comparing two groups. When a homomorphism from \(G\) to \(H\) exists, we might say “some of the algebraic structure in \(H\) resembles the algebraic structure of \(G\text{.}\)” Just how much of the structure in \(H\) resembles \(G\) depends on the range of the function, as well as its ability to tell different elements of its domain apart.

Definition 2.1.12.

Let \(G,H\) be two groups and \(f \colon G \to H\) be a homomorphism.

We say that \(f\) is a trivial homomorphism if, for all \(x\in G\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} f(x) = e, \text{ where }e\text{ is the identity of }H. \end{equation*}

We say that \(f\) is one-to-one if, for all \(x,y\in G\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} \text{If }x\neq y\text{ then } f(x)\neq f(y). \end{equation*}

We say that \(f\) is onto if, for all \(h \in H\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \text{there exists }g \in G\text{ such that }h = f(g). \end{equation*}

If \(f \colon G \to H\) is both one-to-one and onto, we say \(f\) is an isomorphism.

When \(f \colon G \to H\) is an isomorphism, we can say that the algebraic structure of \(H\) completely mirrors the algebraic structure of \(G\text{:}\)

  1. Because \(f\) is one-to-one, each pair of different elements in \(G\) corresponds to a pair of different elements in \(H\text{.}\) (The function \(f\) does not “erase” any differences between elements.)

  2. Because \(f\) is onto, every element in \(H\) is associated with some element of \(G\text{.}\) (The group \(H\) is not “larger” than \(G\text{.}\))

Can we use a homomorphism to compare the structure of braid groups to permutation groups? If we do, what properties can we expect that homomorphism to have?

Definition 2.1.13.

Let \(b \in B_n\) be a braid on \(n\) strands. We say that the permutation induced by \(b\) is the permutation \(F(b) \in S_n\) describing how the top endpoints in the braid \(b\) are permuted into the bottom endpoints.

This definition formalizes our relationship between braids and braid-a-grams discussed earlier in this section. For example, consider the braid \(b = s_2s_1s_3^2s_1 \in B_4\text{.}\) As shown below, the permutation induced by this braid is \(F(b) = (23) \in S_4\text{.}\)

Definition 2.1.15.

A braid \(b \in B_n\) is called a pure braid if its induced permutation \(F(b) = \epsilon \in S_n\) is the trivial permutation.

In other words, a pure braid is one in which each top endpoint ultimately returns to its original position at the bottom endpoint.
